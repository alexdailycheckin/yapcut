# Virality psychology (the scoring lens)

Why things get watched, shared, copied, and remembered. Every script, trend
call, and coined term the radar produces gets scored through this file. The
point is prediction: if we understand the mechanism, we can call what will work
BEFORE the data exists. Grounded principles only, no vibes.

## 1. Attention (the first 2 seconds)

- **Orienting response.** Involuntary attention fires at CHANGE (motion, a new
  voice mid-sentence, an unexpected frame), not at quality. A hook only buys a
  half-second; frame zero must already be mid-action.
- **Habituation / saturation.** The brain down-weights repeated stimuli. The
  feed is ~90% viral formula now, so the formulas ARE the wallpaper. At
  saturation, sincerity and specificity are the pattern interrupt. This is why
  we detect trends EARLY (ride the first wave) or create our own: copying a
  peaked mechanic is camouflage, not reach.
- **Processing fluency.** Easy-to-process beats clever. Short words, one idea,
  a hook a viewer can repeat back after one hearing. If the text hook takes two
  fixations to read, it lost.

## 2. Holding (the retention curve)

- **Zeigarnik effect / curiosity gap.** Unfinished thoughts occupy working
  memory. Open a loop in line one, do not resolve it in line two, stack a
  second loop before paying the first. Real situations carry natural tension,
  which is why honest premises out-hold manufactured ones.
- **Variable reward pacing.** A new "hit" (number, reveal, turn, visual event)
  every few seconds. The editor enforces the visual half (retention_check);
  the script must supply the verbal half: no beat that merely restates.
- **Peak-end rule.** People judge the whole video by its peak and its ending.
  End ON the payoff (the button), never after it. A loop ending (last line
  points back at the first) makes rewatches register, the cheapest
  watch-through multiplier there is.

## 3. Memory (what sticks)

- **Von Restorff (isolation) effect.** The item that differs from its
  neighbours is the one remembered. The creator's specific life and real opinions are
  the difference; borrowed particulars ring false.
- **Specificity = credibility (particular-is-universal).** "I felt bad" slides
  off; "I closed my laptop at 2pm on a Tuesday and felt guilty" lands. Every
  claim carries a number, a name, or a date.
- **Concreteness + naming.** Ideas with a NAME are retrievable; unnamed ideas
  evaporate. This is the whole case for coined terms (see section 5).

## 4. Sharing (Berger's levers, applied)

A view is the algorithm's vote; a share/send is a human's. Scripts should know
WHICH lever they pull:
- **Social currency.** Sharing this makes the sharer look smart, early, or
  inside. "I found this before you" is a gift. Early trend calls and named
  frameworks are social currency machines.
- **Identity signalling.** People share what says who they are. A take that
  lets the viewer signal something they want to be known for travels through
  exactly the audience the creator wants.
- **High-arousal emotion.** Awe, amusement, and indignation spread; contentment
  and mild interest do not. Contrarian takes work because indignation is
  high-arousal, but the take must be defensible (the creator's name is on it).
- **Practical value.** "Steal this" how-tos get SAVED and SENT. Saves and sends
  are the strongest algorithmic signals. This is why a healthy share of the mix teaches a
  concrete do-this.
- **Engineered debate / ambivalence.** A video people argue UNDER gets
  distributed BY the arguers. Build in one deliberately contestable line and
  know in advance what the two camps will say. Ambivalence ("I love this and
  it worries me") outperforms flat certainty because both camps claim it.

## 5. Trends (why formats and terms spread, and how to make one)

A trend is a TEMPLATE other people can perform. Three conditions, all
required:
- **Copyable skeleton.** A fixed, obvious structure (signature opening line,
  2-4 beats, a slot for YOUR example). If a viewer cannot imagine making their
  own episode in 10 seconds, it will not spread.
- **Low effort, high identity fit.** Performing the template must be cheap
  (talking head, no props) and must let the performer signal something they
  want to signal.
- **A handle.** Trends need a NAME (a series title, a coined term, a
  catchphrase). The name is what travels through comments and captions.
  Labelling a fuzzy shared experience ("this could've been an email") claims
  ownership of a feeling everyone had but nobody had named. Coined terms work
  the same way: name a problem people already feel, and every future mention
  of the problem cites you (also the AI-search play: engines cite the coiner).

**Predicting a trend = catching condition timing.** A mechanic is worth riding
when it is rising but not yet wallpaper (first ~2 weeks). Signals it is early:
small accounts overperforming with it, comments still saying "new format?",
no brand accounts using it yet. Signal it is dead: brands and LinkedIn are
doing it.

## 6. Credibility (why they believe you)

- **Costly signals.** Claims that cost something to make are believed:
  receipts on screen (real screenshots, real dashboards), predictions in
  public with a date, admitting what did not work. Cheap talk is discounted.
- **Being early, on record.** A dated "this will happen" clip is a credibility
  annuity: right = authority compounding, wrong = honesty compounding (if you
  revisit it on camera). Either way it beats never calling anything.
- **Teach from the operating table.** "Here's how I actually do it" carries
  first-person operator proof no aggregated listicle can fake (pull the moves from the workspace `methods.md`).

## 7. The scoring checklist (apply to EVERY script and trend call)

Answer these five. Fewer than two strong answers = cut or rework the script.
1. **First 2s:** which attention principle fires, concretely? (What changes on
   screen, what loop opens in the first line?)
2. **Held how:** which loop stays open past the midpoint, and what is the
   variable-reward beat rhythm?
3. **Shared why:** which Berger lever, and WHO shares it to signal WHAT?
4. **Argued about:** what will the two camps in the comments say? (If nobody
   would push back, there is no energy.)
5. **Kept why:** what does the viewer walk away able to DO or SAY (the
   teachable step, or the coined phrase they will repeat)?

Record the result in each script's `psych` field: 1-3 principle names + one
line on why this will work. A script whose `psych` is empty fails QA.
